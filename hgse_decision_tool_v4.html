Do I Have Decision-Making Authority?

Walk through each area honestly. This tool helps you determine whether you are the Accountable party in the RACI — and what to do when you're not. Always use your best judgement. When in doubt, consult your manager.

The principle: It's your decision to make unless it falls outside your scope, has significant upstream or downstream impacts, carries financial implications beyond your authorization, involves legal or compliance risk, or raises significant institutional concerns. When unsure — talk to your manager. Make a decision together. Record what that looks like next time.

Step 1 — Evaluate each area
Scope
This decision falls within my defined role — my position description, current work plan, or an established workflow for Admissions or Financial Aid operations.
Unsure? Check your work plan or consult the RACI matrix tabs.
Financial
Any financial implications — aid award levels, budget expenditures, institutional funding — fall within my authorized level and the department's approved budget plan.
Includes aid reallocation, fellowship awarding, recruitment spend, and scholarship decisions.
Legal / Risk
The decision aligns with HGSE policies, applicable federal and university compliance requirements, FERPA obligations, and standard admissions and financial aid procedures. The institutional risk level is manageable.
If it touches application policy, regulatory compliance, or institutional liability — proceed with caution and consult as needed.
Stakeholders
The impact on co-workers, faculty, applicants, or external partners is non-existent or insignificant. This decision does not involve senior leadership, media, or press.
Would a faculty member, partner organization, or senior leader have a strong opinion? If yes, consider consulting before deciding.
Gut Check
Does making this decision feel right? If the outcome isn't what I hoped, is it reversible? Am I confident making this call right now?
Pause if emotions are high — conflict, volume surges, or time pressure. Create space before deciding. Indecision is still a decision.
Step 2 — Review your result
✅You have decision-making authority — proceed with confidence
You are the Accountable party. Go ahead and make the decision. Keep your manager and relevant colleagues Informed. Your manager will celebrate the process and support your decision — they won't override it without a compelling reason. Feedback will focus on the process and outcome, not the person. Document the decision and incorporate learnings.
↗️Consult before deciding — you are still Accountable
One or more areas need input before you proceed. Identify who will be impacted, bring your analysis and a recommendation, and consult them before finalizing. You remain Accountable. Keep your manager Informed throughout. Document who was consulted and the rationale. This is not slowing down — this is good process.
🔼Escalate — this falls outside your current scope of authority
This is not a decision for which you are currently the Accountable party. You may be the Responsible party who executes once a decision is made. Don't let it stall: bring your analysis and a clear recommendation to your manager, and work together to clarify who holds accountability. Document what this looks like for next time.

Decision Criteria — Quick Reference

Outside scope
Not in your role, work plan, or established workflow → escalate
Upstream / downstream
Significant impact on teammates, faculty, or partners → consult
Financial threshold
Exceeds your authorization or the approved budget → escalate
Legal / compliance
Touches federal regulations, FERPA, or institutional risk → consult or escalate
Brand / reputation
Public-facing or involves senior leadership or major partners → escalate
Not sure?
Talk to your manager. Decide together. Record it for next time.

Decision-Making Philosophy & Values

HGSE Admissions & Financial Aid decision-making is grounded in six core values — shaping not only what we decide, but how we decide and how we hold each other accountable for the process.

Shared Philosophy

Decision-making authority should be grounded in trust, clarity, and a commitment to mission. When a team member makes a decision within their authority — following the criteria in this tool — their manager will celebrate the process and support the decision. They won't override it unless there is a compelling reason. Feedback will focus on process and outcome, not the person. We partner to incorporate learnings and make any needed adjustments together.


"Indecision is still a decision. It's just one you didn't choose. The quality of our work — and the students we serve — is directly tied to the quality of the decisions we are in fact making."

Core Values
🤝
Agency
Team members are empowered to make decisions within their defined scope. Authority is real — we mean it when we say "this is your call."
🔍
Clarity
Roles, criteria, and expectations are transparent. No one should wonder what they "own." When clarity is missing, we name it and fix it together.
🔒
Trust
We trust each other's judgement and process. Managers don't hover; team members don't second-guess their own authority. Trust is built through consistent, documented decisions.
🔁
Consistency
We apply the same criteria to decisions cycle over cycle. Consistency builds credibility — with students, partners, and each other. We document to enable repeatability.
💬
Collaboration
Consulting others is a sign of strength. We actively use our colleagues and partners — especially for high-stakes or novel decisions.
📣
Communication
We keep people Informed even when we don't need their input. Surprises erode trust. A quick update — "I made this call, here's why" — goes a long way.
Shared Accountability Commitments
What team members can expect from managers
  • Celebrate the process. If you make a decision within your authority, your manager will support it and won't override it without a compelling reason.
  • Evaluate together. Your manager will partner with you on impact, adjustments, and learnings — not to assign blame.
  • Focus on process, not person. Feedback addresses how the decision was made and what we learn — never weaponized against a good-faith call.
What managers can expect from team members
  • Own your scope. Proactively identify decisions in your lane and make them — rather than waiting for direction on everything.
  • Keep people informed. Communicate decisions made — especially those with downstream effects — without being asked.
  • Name uncertainty early. If you're unsure whether a decision is yours to make, raise it before the deadline — not after. Bring your analysis and a recommendation.

Decision Health Audit

A 7-question self-assessment adapted from the No Days Off Podcast (Ep. 789). Score yourself honestly: 0 = not yet · 1 = sometimes · 2 = consistently. 14 points total.

"The quality of your work — and ultimately the students you serve — is directly tied to the quality of the decisions you are in fact making. Indecision is still a decision. It's just one you didn't choose."

1
Do you have a clear, repeatable process for approaching significant decisions?
e.g., for admit decisions, aid package design, policy changes, or partnership agreements.
2
Do you actively consult colleagues on hard decisions — and actually use their input?
e.g., partner offices, senior colleagues, faculty, Financial Aid or Admissions counterparts.
3
Before a big call, are you clear on whether the decision is reversible or permanent?
An outreach approach is reversible. A policy change, aid formula shift, or MOU is largely permanent.
4
How often do decisions in your area get made by default — time ran out, the outcome just happened?
A deadline passes, a policy stays ambiguous, an applicant falls through the cracks because no one claimed the call.
5
Are there decisions sitting on your desk right now that you know need to be made — but haven't been?
Name the one you've been sitting on longest. Give yourself a 72-hour deadline and make the call.
6
When emotions are high, can you slow down and make a clear-headed call?
Application volume surges, funding uncertainty, inter-team conflict, difficult individual cases.
7
Do the people on your team clearly know what decisions they are empowered to make without running it through you?
Can your team answer "is this my call?" on their own? If everything runs through you, you are the bottleneck.
